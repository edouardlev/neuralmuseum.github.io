Introduction

This museum will take you through some of Mario Klingemann’s different techniques for creating AI-generated art. Instead of attempting to make art look as good as possible as many artists do, Mario eases into and highlights what makes a GAN imperfect…

ROOM 01 // PERCEPTION GLITCH

Neural Glitch

This is one of the examples where Mario modifies the parameters of GANs, introducing corruption into model weights, or adding in random input data (Górska, 2025).

03 / 12
ROOM 01 // PERCEPTION GLITCH

Neural Decay

This change in the models causes the systems to lose consistent logic, leading to images with clear, imperfect patterns (Górska, 2025). We can see the face seems pixelated and made up of features that don’t seem human-like (Górska, 2025). Klingemann described the art pieces as “a dialogue with a machine that tries to repair itself” (quoted in Górska, 2025).

04 / 12
ROOM 01 // PERCEPTION GLITCH

Uncanny Glitch

We can see how uncanny these generated images become (Górska, 2025). The generated faces do not seem to be quite human, but the machine making them simply does not know better… (Górska, 2025).

05 / 12
ROOM 02 // MEMORY SIMULATION

Memories of Passersby I

This art collection by Klingemann was unique from his previous pieces; it was trained on paintings from the 17th–19th centuries and would create images in an infinite loop of subtly deformed images (Górska, 2025). It works by having two displays hooked up to a computer designed to look like a chestnut wood console, which runs the model (Górska, 2025).

06 / 12
ROOM 02 // MEMORY SIMULATION

Memories of Passersby GIF

This project’s intention is to mimic how someone remembers a face. All of these images are created without human intervention and are meant to leave the viewer feeling slightly uneasy (Górska, 2025).

07 / 12
ROOM 02 // MEMORY SIMULATION

Autonomy & Authorship

As Mario sees the usage of AI as a creative partner, it makes one wonder when the art continues being created yet no human remains in the equation; is Mario still the artist in all of this? It would be plausible to see the actual art being the model, and the presentation of the infinite loop of images, rather than the images themselves being the art, or at least only being one piece of the art. This style of art seems to have many moving variables, and makes it hard to be concrete (Górska, 2025).

08 / 12
ROOM 03 // THE IMPOSTURE SERIES

do not kill the messenger

In this project, Klingemann trained his models on simplified silhouettes (stick figures) (Górska, 2025). The idea for this project is to show how a machine “constructs and deforms physicality” (quoted in Górska, 2025). Klingemann created over 50,000 different deformed and incomplete photos of humans devoid of identity (Górska, 2025).

09 / 12
ROOM 03 // THE IMPOSTURE SERIES

The butcher’s son (special edition)

Mario’s transhancement style works by hallucinating each detail, layer by layer, which ends up exaggerating the image’s own features and ends up with the images seen here.

10 / 12

Conclusion

To conclude, this virtual museum’s goal was to present the different ways that AI can be embraced as a co-creator when making art. Mario completely ignores the traditional rules of art when making his work, which is what makes it so evocative. With all the opposition to AI in recent times, will his vision continue to hold true?
